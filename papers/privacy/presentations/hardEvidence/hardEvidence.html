2 Unraveling

2.1 A simple example

  1. a manager A privately learns the value of the firm \(v\in V=\{v_1,\dots,v_n\}\) with \(v_1
  2. A can disclose his information to the board B
  3. B pays a bonus to A depending on the expected value of the firm (the higher B expects \(v\) to be the higher the bonus)

\vspace*{1cm}

What is the (perfect Bayesian) equilibrium of the game?

2.2 A more general setup

  • A's payoff is \(\pi(v,b)\) where \(b\) is B's belief (probability distribution over \(V=\{v_1,\dots,v_n\}\)) in stage 3
  1. Assumptions   B_block
    • \(\pi\) increases in \(v\)

    • \(\pi\) "increases" in \(b\): \(\pi(v,b)>\pi(v,b')\) if \(b\neq b'\) first order stochastically dominates \(b'\):

      \[\sum_{v_j\leq v}b(v_j)\leq \sum_{v_j\leq v}b'(v_j)\qquad\text{ for all }v\]

  2. :Bignoreheading:
    • A can certify some subset of \(V\) in stage 2 (the true \(v\) must be in the set he certifies)
      • example: A can certify "\(v\) is in \(\{v_2,v_4\}\)" if \(v=v_2\)
  3. Assumption

    A can certify that the value is at least \(v\), i.e. if \(v=v_i\) then the set \(\{v_i,v_{i+1},\dots,v_n\}\) is certifiable

2.3 Unraveling theorem in the more general setup

  1. Unraveling   B_theorem

    The equilibrium involves full information revelation and pessimistic beliefs.

  2. :Bignoreheading:
    • pessimistic beliefs: for any certified set, B beliefs that \(v\) is the lowest \(v_i\) in the set

    \textbf{Proof. }

3 Partial Unraveling

3.1 Costly verification I

  • say disclosure comes at cost \(c>0\)
  • for which \(v_i\) will A disclose, for which not?

3.2 Costly verification II

  • let \(v'\) be the highest \(\tilde{v}\) that satisfies

\[\hspace*{2cm}\mathbb{E}[v| v\leq \tilde{v}]

  • equilibrium:
    • not disclose for \(v\leq v'\)
    • disclose for \(v> v'\)

3.3 Dye's model I

  • back to simple disclosure: A cannot certify sets but at best the actual \(v\)
  • A knows \(v\) with probability \(q\)
  • A does not know \(v\) with probability \(1-q\)
  • prior belief over \(\{v_1,\dots,v_n\}\) is denoted by \(f\) (with full support)
  • A's payoff is \(\alpha v+\bar{v}\) where \(\alpha\geq 0\) and \(\bar v\) is B's expectation over \(v\) in stage 3
  • same timing as in simple example (note that A cannot disclose anything if he does not know \(v\))

3.4 Dye's model II

  1. partial disclosure   B_example
    • \(V=\{1,2,9\}\) and \(f(v_i)=1/3\) for \(v_i\in V\)
    • \(q=3/4\) and \(\alpha=1\)
    • Equilibrium: A discloses if \(v=9\) and does not disclose otherwise:
      • given this, what is the prob of non-disclosure?
    • if non-disclosure is observed what is probability that A does not know \(v\)?
    • if non-disclosure is observed what is probability that \(v=1\), that \(v=2\)?
    • what is \(\bar{v}\) when observing non-disclosure?
    • what is A's payoff of not disclosing if \(v=2\)?

3.5 Dye's model III

  • let \(v_i\) be defined such that

\[\hspace*{1cm}\mathbb{E}[v|\mbox{ v has no evidence or }v\leq v_i\}] \geq v_i\] \[\hspace*{1cm}\mathbb{E}[v|\mbox{ v has no evidence or }v\leq v_i\}] \leq v_{i+1}\]

  • this \(v_i\) exists
    • LHS is above RHS for \(v_i = v_1\)
    • LHS is below RHS for \(v_i = v_n\)
  1. Partial disclosure   B_theorem

    It is an equilibrium that all types above \(v_i\) disclose and those below do not.

3.6 Dye's model IV

  • say \(v\) is an asset value or stock price that is traded
  • suppose there is a stage 4 where \(v\) becomes known (asset pays out, project finishes, end of year report)
  • how is strategic disclosure different from "disclose everything"?
    • strategic:
      • price of asset drops in stage 2/3 if no disclosure (no news is bad news)
      • without disclosure: uncertainty/volatility about asset value in 4
    • disclose everything:
      • price stays constant (in 2/3) if no news
      • price drops if bad news but then no uncertainty about asset value in 4
  • empirical test for strategic disclosure: does a price drop lead to more volatility?

4 Limited Communication

4.1 Limited Communication I

  • a course has 2 topics
  • student A can either understand or not understand a topic (\(V=\{0,1\}\) for each topic)
  • for each topic the probability of understanding \(f(1)=1/2\) (and values are independent across topics)
  • the exam has limited length: A can communicate at most 1 topic to teacher B
  • with probability \(1-q=1/4\) there is miscommunication and the teacher cannot understand/receive what the student says in the exam (even if A understands the topic)
  • A gets a grade equal to B's ex post expected number of topics A has understood

4.2 Limited Communication II

  • what is the probability that "understanding" can be communicated if A chooses the topic of the exam?
  • what is B's belief in case \(v=1\) is (not) communicated if A chooses the topic?
  • what is the probability that "understanding" can be communicated if B chooses the topic of the exam randomly?
  • what is B's belief in case \(v=1\) is (not) communicated if B chooses the topic randomly?
  • does the grade distribution have more variance if A or if B chooses the topic?
  • who should choose the topic if the grades should be as informative as possible about the the student's understanding?

5 An investment example (BDL)

5.1 Investment decisions I

  • say A can choose between two technologies
    • technology 1 gives \(v=4\) for sure
    • technology 2 gives \(v=6\) and \(v=0\) with probability 1/2 each
  • A's objective is to maximize \(\bar{v}\) (i.e. \(\alpha=0\) for simplicity)
  • A has evidence with probability \(q\) as before
  • the value realizes in stage 4 and goes to B

\vspace*{0.5cm}

  • What is the efficient technology choice?

5.2 Investment decisions II

  • Is it an equilibrium to choose technology 1?
    • in such an equilibrium what is \(\bar{v}\) without disclosure?
  • what is A's payoff when he chooses technology 2?
  • Is it an equilibrium to choose technology 2?
    • what will A disclose in such an equilibrium?
  • in such an equilibrium what is \(\bar{v}\) without disclosure?
  • what is A's expected payoff?
  • what is A's expected payoff from deviating to technology 1?

5.3 Investment decisions III

  • general result: A engages in excessive risk taking
    • enjoy upside risk
    • hide downside risk by not disclosing
    • roughly:
      • \(\hat v\) be the belief if no disclosure
      • A chooses \(\max\{v,\hat v\}\) → convex function of \(v\)
      • A is risk lover

6.2 Exercise I

An entrepreneur (E) has to sell his project to a perfectly competitive and risk neutral financial market before it matures. With probability \(p\) the project is good and makes a profit of 1. With probability \(1-p\) the project is bad and makes zero profits. With probability \(q\) the entrepreneur finds out whether his project is good before he sells it and in this case he has evidence to document the project value.

  • When will E document the value?
  • What is the market price of the project in case there is (no) evidence?
  • What is the market price of the project in case it is documented to be good?
  • What is the expected market price of the project?
